CHAPTER 1
INTRODUCTION
The World Health Organization (WHO), in collaboration with the United Nations’ Special
Programme in Human Reproduction (HRP) and The Pleasure Projects highlights a crucial yet
often overlooked factor in contraceptive use—its impact on sexual well-being. Analyzing the 64
studies and over 125,000 participants, found that 1 in 20 individuals who discontinue
contraception while still needing it do so because they perceive negative effects on their sex
lives. It also underscores that sexual satisfaction plays a significant role in contraceptive
continuation and overall reproductive health. When people have access to contraceptive
methods that align with both their physical and sexual needs, it not only promotes consistent
use but also helps reduce unintended pregnancies, maternal mortality, and sexually transmitted
infections (World, 2025).
The Center for Communication Programs in the Philippines is working to understand why
some women stop using contraceptives and how to get them back on track. Findings from the
Demographic and Health Survey found that women who have used contraception in the past
make up about 38% of the total unmet need for contraception across 34 countries. Many of
these women who discontinued contraception were because of misconceptions, misinformation
or uncomfortable side effects associated with certain contraceptive methods. Although some
may wish to use contraception again, they often face barriers such as a lack of accurate
information, limited access to healthcare guidance, or uncertainty about which method would
better suit their needs (Desmon, 2025)
Informed contraceptive choice, or the ability to freely access and choose from methods
of contraception is recognized as a necessary component of reproductive autonomy, understood
as people being able to control and decide their own on when and how to have a child. Users’
preferences for these attributes are the important thing to the process of making informed
contraceptive choices.

Background of the Study
Family planning continues to be an important role in promoting reproductive health and
empowering Filipino women to make informed choices about their fertility and well-being. Over
the years, national programs in the Philippines have evolved from simply providing
contraceptive supplies to implementing more holistic approaches that emphasize client-centered
counseling and consideration of women’s medical eligibility and preferences. Despite these
advancements, significant challenges remain. The 2022 National Demographic and Health
Survey (NDHS) reported that 65% of currently married women use some form of contraception,
where 50% are modern methods and 15% are traditional methods. However, nearly one in three
women aged 15-49 discontinue contraceptive use within 12 months, most commonly due to side
effects, health concerns, or method dissatisfaction. (Philippine Statistics Authority [PSA] & ICF,
2022). These discontinuation rates contribute to unmet contraceptive needs, unintended
pregnancies, and, in some cases, unsafe abortions that lead to health consequences.
There are several family planning methods available to Filipino women, ranging from
traditional approaches such as withdrawal, periodic abstinence, and the Standard Days Method,
as well as modern methods like pills, injectables, intrauterine devices (IUDs), implants, and
sterilization. Modern contraceptives are globally recognized as the most effective, with
typical-use failure rates below 9%, compared to over 20% among traditional methods (Bertrand
et al., 2021). However, many Filipino women continue to rely on traditional methods due to
limited access to healthcare facilities and strong cultural or religious influences (Laksono et al.,
2022; Flores et al., 2025). This continued dependence on less effective methods, together with
high discontinuation rates, hinders the progress of the Philippines toward achieving universal
access to reproductive health services and sustainable family planning.
The 2022 National Demographic and Health Survey (NDHS) conducted by the Philippine
Statistics Authority (PSA) and ICF International (2023) revealed that the top contraceptive
3
methods used by Filipino women are pills, injectables, female sterilization, and male condoms.
The pill remains the most widely used method, chosen by 20% of married women and 7% of
unmarried women, with most obtaining it from private medical sectors. Injectables (9%) and
female sterilization (9%) follow as the next most common methods, primarily accessed through
the public sector. On the other hand, male condoms are the leading modern method among
sexually active unmarried women (12%), providing protection against both pregnancy and
sexually transmitted infections. Despite the availability of these modern contraceptives, around
17–18% of women continue to rely on traditional methods such as withdrawal and rhythm.
These findings emphasize that while modern contraceptive use dominates family planning
practices in the Philippines, there remains a crucial need to further strengthen access,
awareness, and education on effective and safe family planning methods (Philippine Statistics
Authority (PSA) & ICF, 2023).
From the healthcare provider’s perspective, family planning counseling often faces
challenges such as time constraints, limited access to digital tools, and difficulty in continuously
monitoring patients after initiation (Dominguez et al., 2023).Providers are required to balance
medical eligibility, personal preferences, and side-effect considerations while promoting
informed choice which is a task that is complex. Moreover, many healthcare facilities,
particularly in rural and geographically isolated areas of the Philippines, experience unstable or
limited internet connectivity, hindering the consistent use of web-based health systems
(Department of Information and Communications Technology [DICT], 2023). Studies have
shown that offline-capable mobile health applications can help overcome connectivity barriers
by allowing providers to access decision support tools even without internet access (Afolabi et
al., 2021; Omondi et al., 2022). On the other hand, patients frequently struggle with
misconceptions, fear of side effects, and weak follow-up systems, which contribute to premature
4
contraceptive discontinuation (Ontiri et al., 2021). Therefore, a mobile application that operates
offline offers a practical and sustainable solution for both healthcare providers and patients,
enabling risk assessment even in limited connectivity places.
The World Health Organization’s Medical Eligibility Criteria for Contraceptive Use (MEC)
serves as an important framework that provides evidence-based recommendations to ensure
the medical safety of contraceptive selection. It assists healthcare providers in identifying which
contraceptive methods are appropriate for women based on their individual health conditions
and risk factors. The MEC has been widely adopted in many countries, including the
Philippines, to standardize family planning counseling and reduce the risk of adverse outcomes
(WHO, 2022). However, while the MEC effectively ensures medical safety, it does not assess
factors influencing method continuation. This shows the need for complementary tools that
integrate predictive analytics to assess discontinuation risk.
Decision Tree models are widely used in healthcare applications for their ability to
generate clear, rule-based classifications making them suitable when classifying binary
outcomes such as “discontinue” vs. “continue” contraceptive use. For example, Zeng & Cheng
(2021) highlights how decision trees offer flexibility and strong interpretability when classifying
medical data, noting that internal nodes represent decision rules based on features (e.g., side
effect occurrence, patient age, partner influence) and leaf nodes map directly to the target class.
In a study of hematologic disorders, tree-based classification achieved higher accuracy and
provided easily understandable decision rules compared to conventional indices (Rahim et al.,
2021).
These models allow healthcare professionals to trace exactly how input factors lead to a
given output, which is critical for clinical environments that emphasize transparency and trust.
However, decision trees also face known drawbacks, especially the risk of overfitting to training
data when the tree grows too deep or when numerous features with many levels are used. This
5
can lower the performance on diverse datasets if not appropriately constrained by simplifying
the model, limiting depth, or feature selection. Thus, while decision trees can serve as a strong
foundation for binary classification in the proposed system, it is important to carefully balance
their interpretability with model design practices to ensure consistent, reliable prediction in real
world settings.
On the other hand, eXtreme Gradient Boosting, an ensemble algorithm, has
demonstrated superior performance in structured health datasets by combining many weak
learners, applying regularization, and managing missing data more effectively. Yehuala (2024)
applied the XGBoost algorithm to reproductive health data and reported higher discrimination
and robustness compared to other models such as Random Forest, Support Vector Machine,
and Decision Tree. However, XGBoost’s complexity and many tuning requirements make its
logic harder to explain to non-technical users such as clinicians.
Given these complementary strengths and weaknesses, this study proposes a hybrid
approach that merges the interpretability of Decision Trees with the predictive strength of
XGBoost. Through this hybrid integration, the system aims to provide an app-based decision
support tool capable of predicting contraceptive discontinuation risk while remaining
interpretable enough for healthcare providers to trust and act upon its recommendations. This
model, incorporated within the system, seeks to complement the World Health Organization’s
Medical Eligibility Criteria (MEC) by first ensuring medical suitability and then identifying users at
higher risk of discontinuation. This not only complements the MEC tool by enhancing its
predictive capability but also provides both healthcare providers and patients with an accessible,
evidence-based platform for informed contraceptive decision-making.

Project Objectives
The general objective of this study is to develop a mobile-based contraceptive decision
support system that utilizes machine learning algorithms to predict the risk of contraceptive
discontinuation and provide data-driven recommendations that reduce discontinuation and
support contraceptive use.
The specific objectives of this study are as follows:
1. To utilize Decision Tree and eXtreme Gradient Boosting algorithms for accurately
predicting contraceptive discontinuation risk through binary classification of users as Low
Risk or High Risk for discontinuation.
2. To implement a data processing and model integration pipeline that analyzes users’
demographic, health, lifestyle, and contraceptive-related data to identify significant
predictors of contraceptive discontinuation and generate risk estimates in alignment with
Medical Eligibility Criteria (MEC) guidelines.
3. To develop a mobile-based decision support system featuring a dual-module interface
consisting of a user module that enable self-assessment and contraceptive
recommendations, and an OB-GYN module that enables healthcare professionals to
review assessment results, interpret risk of discontinuation, and provide professional
guidance with detailed contraceptive information to support informed choices.
4. To evaluate the quality of the mobile application using ISO/IEC 25010 standard metrics
such as functional suitability, performance efficiency, interaction capability,
maintainability, and compatibility.

Scope and Delimitations
Scope
This study focuses on the development of a contraceptive decision support system that
combines two distinct algorithms: Decision Tree and eXtreme Gradient Boosting into one
hybrid model that is able to predict the binary outcome of discontinuation: true or false, and its
probability. The main objective is to assist individuals in making informed choices regarding
contraceptive methods based on their personal health profiles, preferences, and accessibility to
products. The study will use the Medical Eligibility Criteria (MEC) Wheel to identify and filter
9
contraceptive methods that are medically safe and aligned with the patient’s preferences and
health conditions. Furthermore, the system will provide a consistent reminder for users to
consult licensed healthcare professionals before making any contraceptive decisions based on
the recommendations generated by the MEC tool within the application. Additionally, Quezon
City has been selected as the initial implementation site for the proposed application, utilizing its
status as the most populous city in the Philippines and its recorded count of 277,872 family
planning users.
Security will be implemented through authentication and the implementation of
role-based access control. The mobile application will have three defined roles: user, expert,
and administrator. Patients, or users, will be able to access only the basic functionalities of the
system without the need to log in. This includes access to the MEC tool, where age and
preferences serve as required inputs for determining the appropriateness of a contraceptive
method. The login functionality will be exclusive to expert and administrator roles. Expert
accounts are intended for licensed medical professionals authorized to provide family planning.
Authentication is specifically designed to verify these professionals and enable them to perform
secure storing of records. Authentication will be manually verified through the Professional
Regulation Commission (PRC) using the license verification method, which requires the
professional’s name, license number, and birthdate. These details will be among the required
information during registration in the system as a medical professional.This also ensures that
clients cannot self-diagnose or access professional tools without proper credentials.
Administrator accounts are assigned to the system developers, who are responsible for
performing updates, maintenance, and managing the technical aspects of the application. They
will have access to advanced system features, including database management and system
configuration.
Hybrid prediction modeling will utilize two algorithms: the CART Decision Tree and
eXtreme Gradient Boosting. The algorithms are evaluated using classification metrics: accuracy,
10
recall, AUC-ROC, and F1-score. The hybrid model built from both of these algorithms used
ensemble techniques, specifically sklearn’s VotingClassifier, to determine the appropriate
output. It will employ hard voting when soft voting is not supported. The model is evaluated via
10-fold stratified cross-validation, calculating mean and standard deviation for accuracy, recall,
F1-score, and AUC-ROC, with recall prioritized to assess optimal performance. For clinical
implementation, a fixed 0.50 probability threshold, as recommended by the OBGYN
Reproductive Endocrinologist and Infertility (REI) specialist, is used to classify users as “High
Risk” or “Low Risk.” The system provides two primary outputs. The first is the predictive
outcome, which provides a categorical risk classification together with its probability score. The
second is an interpretability report, generated using SHAP, which quantifies how individual
features such as socioeconomic status, age, method type, education level, and usage intention
contribute to increasing or decreasing a user's predicted risk. This SHAP-based analysis
provides a transparent decision making by detailing the factors influencing each classification.
MEC tool is a criteria developed by WHO to standardize the assessment of
contraceptive methods. The results of the criteria are directly dependent on the conditions and
preferences of the user. Specifically, the contraceptive methods that will be included are:
combined hormonal contraception (CHC) such as the contraceptive patch, progestogen-only
pills (POP) like Norethindrone, progestogen-only injectables (DMPA) such as Depot
Medroxyprogesterone Acetate, progesterone-only implants (LNG/ETG) such as Etonogestrel
implant, levonorgestrel IUD (LNG-IUD) like Mirena or Kyleena, and the Paragard for copper
intrauterine device (Cu-IUD). The MEC tool’s assessments will be entirely based on the
standardized conditions and classifications provided in the WHO framework.
The mobile application will be developed with React Native 0.81.4 along with
Typescript 5.9.2 for type safety. Compatibility will be limited to the Android platform. The
client-side part of the application will aid informed choice for contraceptive methods. Upon
11
information input from the user, the application will fetch the stored JSON containing the
matched usability based on conditions and preferences. If for example the user inputted two
different conditions, the lower rating will be displayed. The system is an offline-first architecture
meaning that the models are stored locally. When connected to the internet the system will
check for any unsynced data and will sync as needed. This ensures that the application will be
available even in places with little to no internet connection. The evaluation for the system will
be based on ISO/IEC 25010 specifically the criterias functional suitability, performance
efficiency, interaction capability, maintainability, and compatibility. Lastly, the application will be
deployed at Google Playstore. It will be called ContraceptIQ similar to the title of this study and
will undergo a closed test with at least 20 voluntary testers who will try the application within 14
days. Additionally, it must align with the policies of Google Playstore and must be stable,
engaging, and responsive.
Delimitations
The system will not require basic users to create accounts or log in to access the
application. The login functionality will be limited to expert and administrator roles only. Basic
users will not handle sensitive or identifiable data, therefore, user level access will not involve
local data storage or encryption requirements.
The hybrid prediction model will not employ additional machine learning algorithms
beyond Decision Tree and eXtreme Gradient Boosting. Other ensemble techniques such as
Random Forest, AdaBoost, or Bagging, as well as deep learning models like neural networks or
convolutional architectures, will not be included in the implementation. Parameter optimization
will be constrained to model tuning within the chosen algorithms.
The system will not accommodate traditional contraceptive methods, nor will it include
conditions, criteria, or modern methods that are not officially recognized by the World Health
Organization (WHO) Medical Eligibility Criteria (MEC) guidelines. Any emerging or specific to
12
region contraceptive options, off-label uses, or unlisted medical conditions will not be analyzed,
processed, or predicted by the system.
The mobile application will not function as a replacement for professional consultation.
The generated outputs will not serve as medical prescriptions or definitive medical advice, but
as supplementary decision-support information for professionals. The study does not involve
real-time clinical testing or patient trials, and therefore will not assess medical outcomes, side
effects, or behavioral responses of users following the system’s suggestions.
The mobile application will not be developed for iOS or other operating systems and no
web or desktop versions will be deployed. Furthermore, the application will not support
continuous real-time server communication, instead, it will operate primarily offline, with
synchronization limited to available internet connectivity. Integration with external APIs, hospital
management systems, or electronic health record (EHR) systems will not be implemented.
The study will not include the development of a backend server for real-time analytics or
cloud-based computation. Data storage will be stored to local or device-level environments
during testing.

Review of Related Literature
Contraceptive Use and Discontinuation
Contraceptive use in the Philippines has increased by 58% from 1993 to 2022, yet
discontinuation remains a challenge for family planning providers. The 2022 National
Demographic and Health Survey found that one in three Filipino women aged 15-49
discontinued a contraceptive method within 12 months of initiation.
High rates of contraceptive discontinuation hinder progress in family planning programs,
as pregnancies following method-related discontinuation are frequently reported as unintended,
contributing significantly to unmet contraceptive needs (Gemmill et al., 2023).
Recent evidence shows that portions of contraceptive discontinuations occur within the
initial three months of use, with bleeding disturbances accounting for 48.2% of these cases.
Such menstrual changes often subside within three to five months, suggesting the importance of
comprehensive counseling on potential side effects and their expected duration to mitigate
premature discontinuation (Compton et al., 2023).
14
The leading reasons for discontinuation of modern methods were side effects or health
concerns, reported by almost half of IUD users (48%), injectable users (47%), implant users
(42%), and pill users (31%).By contrast, discontinuation of traditional methods such as the
rhythm method (33%) and withdrawal (34%) was mostly due to unintended pregnancies
(Philippine Statistics Authority [PSA] & ICF, 2022).
Studies on long-acting reversible contraceptives (LARCs) show similar trends, with
69.8% of 1,050 women discontinuing use before the recommended duration and 30.2%
switching to other modern methods, primarily short-acting contraceptives (38.8%). The main
reasons for discontinuation and switching were method-specific side effects and the desire to
become pregnant, and women with only one child were 1.61 times more likely to discontinue
than those with five or more children, highlighting the influence of parity on continuation (Bereku
et al., 2022).
These findings emphasize that side effects, health concerns, and limited counseling on
expected physical changes are major factors of discontinuation, particularly within the first few
months of use. These findings call for interventions that extend beyond ensuring medical
eligibility, emphasizing the importance of discontinuation risk assessment to support sustained
contraception. Integrating predictive tools into family planning programs could help healthcare
providers to personalize counseling and ultimately improve contraceptive continuation rates.
Factors Influencing Contraceptive Discontinuation
A secondary analysis of Demographic and Health Survey (DHS) data from nine
high-fertility rate countries was examined by Coulibaly et al. (2025) to determine the factors
associated with contraceptive discontinuation among lactating women with children under 24
months of age. Analyzing data from 5,599 lactating women collected between 2018-2021 were
employed to identify individual and community-level factors associated with contraceptive
discontinuation. Side effects with 29.1% emerged as the primary reason for discontinuation,
followed by 24.7% due to lack of partner or rare sexual activity, 7.9% desire for more effective
15
methods, and 7.5% for wanting to become pregnant. The multivariate analysis revealed that the
shorting method was the strongest predictor showing significantly higher discontinuation rates
than long-term contraceptives.
Lama et al. (2024) conducted a study examining the factors associated with hormonal
contraceptive discontinuation among women of childbearing age attending the La Libertad
Health Center in Lima, Peru. The study found that educational level, distance to health centers,
experiencing adverse events, and inadequate provider counseling were all significantly
associated with discontinuation. Women who experienced adverse events with hormonal
contraceptives showed a prevalence ratio of 26.38 for discontinuation using bivariate analysis.
More importantly, when all variables were introduced to the multivariate analysis regression
model, adverse events remained the only independent factor with an adjusted prevalence ratio
of 3.33. This finding underscores that despite the influence of educational, geographic, and
healthcare system factors, the experience of side effects represents the major reason for
hormonal contraceptive discontinuation.
Marista et al. (2022) review that multiple factors associated with contraceptive
discontinuation were marital status, education, counseling quality, age, parity, occupation,
contraceptive satisfaction, partner support, desire for pregnancy, and side effects. The study
shows that health problems and side effects were the most common reason for Long-Acting
Reversible Contraceptive (LARC) discontinuation. The findings highlight the critical need for
healthcare workers to provide comprehensive counseling about common side effects and
effective management strategy to reduce discontinuation rates and prevent unintended
pregnancies.
The mentioned studies collectively highlight that contraceptive discontinuation is a
multifactorial issue influenced by individual, social, and healthcare related factors. These
underscore the importance of improving patient education and addressing unfavorable
experiences proactively. For the current study, understanding these factors supports the
16
integration of predictive analytics into a contraceptive discontinuation prediction allowing
healthcare providers to identify high-risk users early, tailor counseling to individual needs, and
promote better continuation outcomes in reproductive health programs.
Decision-Support and Counseling Frameworks in Family Planning
Decision support tools have emerged as a promising strategy to facilitate shared
decision-making in contraceptive counseling. Such tools help women clarify their preferences,
compare methods, and communicate effectively with OB professionals ultimately supporting
method continuation and reproductive goal achievement. The World Health Organization
developed Medical Eligibility Criteria (MEC) for Contraceptive Use, an evidence-based guidance
used as a decision support tool for OB professionals and users which assess the eligibility,
discuss methods, and make informed choices to determine which contraceptive methods are
suitable for users given her specific medical and personal conditions.
Decision support interventions such as patient decision aids and general patient
education materials improved individuals’ knowledge of contraceptive methods, strengthened
their confidence in the option they selected, and reduced decisional conflict. These tools support
informed and value-consistent choices, thereby improving the overall quality of the
contraceptive decision-making process (De Bortoli et al., 2024).
In the study of Meekers et al. (2024) Six tools have been developed to support family
planning counselling functioning as a decision support tool. Although existing tools are effective
in supporting information exchange and decision-making, they have different limitations. These
include insufficient guidance in dealing uncertainties, lack of provider-patient relationship, and
enabling self-management. While these tools show promise in aligning contraceptive
counselling with clients’ goals and preferences, more rigorous and context-sensitive research is
needed to establish their impact on contraceptive uptake, satisfaction, and continuation.
The reviewed literature emphasizes that decision-support frameworks play a crucial role
in improving the quality and personalization of contraceptive counseling. However, despite their
17
effectiveness, existing decision aids often lack additional features that consider contraceptive
continuation . This underscores the need for systems that not only align with MEC guidance but
also predict discontinuation risks, thereby empowering healthcare providers to offer proactive,
tailored counseling and improve contraceptive continuation outcomes.
Contraceptive Decision Aids and Digital Tools
Choosing the right contraceptive method can be difficult, especially because there are
many options, each with different ingredients, ways of use, and goals. Some are short-term like
pills or condoms, while others are long-acting like IUDs or injectables. Many people struggle to
pick the best one for their body and lifestyle. That’s why decision aids are helpful tools. They
give users clear, personalized information to help them make smarter and more confident
choices.
Today, many of these aids are made using digital technology like apps or websites.
These tools are more interactive and easier to use than printed brochures or standard
counseling alone. For example, a 2022 study by Goueth et al. reviewed many technology-based
decision aids and found that users of these tools were more likely to continue using their chosen
contraceptive method. This means tech-based aids help people stick with what works best for
them.
Another tool called “My Contraceptive Choice” was tested on young women. It asked
questions about the user’s health, lifestyle, and concerns, and then suggested methods that fit
best. After using the tool, 88% of users said they learned more about birth control, and 91% said
it was easy to use. It was also very accurate matching the right method with user preferences in
about 72% of cases (Redman et al., 2022).
The reviewed study shows that choosing the right contraceptive can be challenging due
to the variety of options available and individual needs. Decision tools like apps and websites
provide clear, personalized guidance that helps users make informed choices. Studies indicate
that technology-based aids improve method continuation rates (Goueth et al., 2022) and
18
enhance user knowledge and satisfaction. Tools such as My Contraceptive Choice
demonstrated high usability and effectively matched methods to user preferences (Redman et
al., 2022).
Binary Outcome in Healthcare
Adem et al. (2025) utilized explainable machine learning algorithms to model women’s
intention to use family planning in Ethiopia, classifying whether respondents intended to use
contraceptive methods or not. The study compared algorithms such as Logistic Regression,
Random Forest, AdaBoost, XGBoost, and SVM, reporting that Random Forest achieved the
highest predictive accuracy of 77% with an AUC of 0.65. Their findings show that interpretable
machine learning models can effectively handle binary health outcomes while providing
transparency through feature importance and decision explanations.
Badawy et al. (2023) discussed the application of machine learning and deep learning
techniques in healthcare predictive analytics, emphasizing that many clinical problems are
formulated as binary classification tasks such as the presence or absence of a disease,
treatment success or failure, or patient adherence versus discontinuation. Their study
highlighted the effectiveness of algorithms such as Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, Random
Forest, and Support Vector Machine (SVM) in modeling binary health outcomes due to their
capacity to uncover complex and nonlinear patterns in clinical data. These models have
improved early diagnosis, personalized treatment, and risk assessment which are key aspects
of preventive and precision medicine. However, Badawy et al. also emphasized persistent
challenges in data imbalance, quality, and interpretability, stressing the importance of adopting
explainable AI models to build clinician trust and ensure transparency in healthcare predictions.
Agbana et al. (2022) focused specifically on contraceptive discontinuation among
Nigerian women of reproductive age using data from the 2018 Nigeria Demographic and Health
Survey (NDHS). The researchers applied binary logistic regression to identify key predictors of
discontinuation and found that over 60% of respondents discontinued family planning methods
19
for reasons unrelated to pregnancy. The main factors influencing discontinuation included side
effects (15.2%), method failure (12.9%), education level, religion, number of children,
employment status, and regional differences. This study demonstrates the practical use of
binary modeling to reveal critical determinants of contraceptive behavior and discontinuation
risks.
These studies establish that binary outcome modeling is a powerful approach in
healthcare predictive analytics, particularly for reproductive health applications such as
contraceptive use and discontinuation. The works of Adam et al. (2025) and Badawy et al.
(2023) demonstrate how machine learning algorithms can enhance prediction accuracy and
support decision-making, while Agbana et al. (2022) provides empirical evidence of the key
socio-demographic and behavioral predictors associated with contraceptive discontinuation.
Despite their effectiveness, the recurring issues of data imbalance and model interpretability
underscore the necessity of hybrid and explainable approaches, such as combining
interpretable models like Decision Trees with performance-oriented models like XGBoost, to
achieve both accuracy and transparency in predictive reproductive health systems.
Machine Learning and Algorithmic Approaches in Predictive Healthcare
Machine learning has emerged as a powerful approach to enhance contraceptive
counseling and predict user outcomes. Population-based surveys, such as the Demographic
and Health Surveys (DHS), traditionally rely on multinomial logistic regression to examine how
demographic and socioeconomic factors like age, parity, education, and wealth shape
contraceptive choices.
Recent advancements in predictive analytics have demonstrated the potential of
machine learning algorithms in improving health-related decision support. A study applied seven
classification algorithms which are AdaBoost, XGB Classifier, Random Forest (RF), Naive
Bayes, Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LGBM), Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost), and
Decision Tree to predict informed contraceptive choice among women in Sub-Saharan African
20
countries. Ensemble methods like boosting and bagging helped avoid overfitting, reflecting
hybrid modeling principles that integrate multiple algorithms for robust predictions. Among
these, the LGBM achieved the highest predictive accuracy of 73%, with an AUC of 0.80,
precision of 71, and recall of 77, effectively identifying influential factors for informed
contraceptive choice (Melaku et al., 2025).
The application of explainable artificial intelligence in healthcare continues to expand. An
explainable Decision Tree model was implemented to predict breast cancer malignancy,
outperforming other models such as Discriminant Analysis, Logistic Regression, Support Vector
Machines (SVM), Naive Bayes, K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), Ensemble Learning, and Artificial
Neural Networks (ANN), with an accuracy of 91.7%. The study further enhanced interpretability
by integrating SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) to visualize feature importance (Akben &
Yumrutaş, 2025). Similarly, Wiens et al. (2025) discussed the broad applicability of machine
learning algorithms, particularly XGBoost, in predicting clinical outcomes such as patient risk
and treatment response. Logistic regression remains widely used for its interpretability, though it
often struggles to capture complex nonlinear relationships within datasets (Prachi et al., 2025).
Recent analyses of large-scale datasets have compared predictive performance across
algorithms in contraceptive behavior research. Using DHS data from nine high-fertility countries,
researchers identified key determinants of contraceptive discontinuation among lactating
women. Side effects, lack of partner involvement, and preference for more effective methods
emerged as major predictors, with short-term methods showing significantly higher
discontinuation rates than long-term ones (Coulibaly et al., 2025).
Modhugua and Ponnusamy (2024) demonstrated that among SVM, Logistic
Regression, and Decision Tree models for liver disease prediction, the SVM achieved the
highest recall, precision, and F1-score (0.85), indicating its robustness for clinical classification
tasks.
21
The increasing role of decision tree-based models in medical prediction has also been
noted. Comparative studies have demonstrated that ensemble and tree-based methods such as
CART and C5.0 can outperform traditional regression approaches under specific conditions. A
comparative evaluation of Decision Tree algorithms—CART, C5.0, and CHAID—against Logistic
Regression for predicting COVID-19 mortality found that CART achieved a recall of 75%, higher
than other models, underscoring its suitability in contexts where minimizing false negatives is
crucial (Mohammadi-Pirouz et al., 2024).
In a separate analysis, multiple algorithms including logistic regression, random forests,
naïve Bayes, LASSO, classification trees, AdaBoost, and neural networks were tested to predict
contraceptive practices. The neural network model outperformed the others, achieving 79.34%
accuracy, an 83.44% F1-score, and an AUC of 86.90, underscoring its potential in contraceptive
decision support (Haq et al., 2023). Another predictive modeling study on contraceptive
discontinuation utilized eight algorithms such as Logistic Regression, Random Forest, KNN,
Artificial Neural Network, Support Vector Machine, Naive Bayes, Extreme Gradient Boosting,
and AdaBoost—trained with tenfold cross-validation and SMOTE balancing. Random Forest
produced the highest accuracy of 67.9% and an AUC of 0.74, identifying age, fertility
preference, decision-making dynamics, employment, and husband’s education as critical
predictors (Kebede et al., 2023).
Methodologically, logistic regression remains an essential foundation in clinical and
social research due to its interpretability and capacity to analyze binary outcomes. However, its
limitations in handling complex, nonlinear relationships have driven the rise of tree-based and
ensemble approaches. Studies have further emphasized the importance of proper model
validation, handling of missing data, and sample size determination to ensure reliable,
generalizable predictive systems (Battista et al., 2023; Zabor et al., 2022; Arista, 2022).
The reviewed studies highlight that machine learning offers greater accuracy and
predictive power than statistical models in understanding contraceptive discontinuation. While
22
multinomial logistic regression remains useful for analyzing demographic and socioeconomic
influences, advanced ML models such as neural networks, random forests, and
gradient-boosted trees better capture complex, non-linear relationships. Haq et al. (2023) and

improving contraceptive decision support. Additionally, Zabor et al. (2022) emphasize proper
model validation and data handling, which are vital for developing reliable and interpretable
predictive systems.
Hybrid Modelling Approaches for Risk Prediction
Recent research highlights the effectiveness of hybrid machine learning models in
enhancing predictive performance across various domains. A study developed a hybrid
framework combining Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and Random Search (LR + RF +
RS), which achieved significantly higher accuracy and robustness than the individual models
alone. This hybrid approach was particularly effective in handling imbalanced data, obtaining an
accuracy of 0.9574, precision of 0.9665, recall of 0.9576, and a high F1-score. The findings
demonstrated the model’s applicability in scenarios like student grade prediction, where uneven
data distribution is common (Rohman et al., 2025).
Sindhuja et al. (2025) used a hybrid XGBoost and Decision Tree model for Intrusion
Detection Systems, achieving the highest accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 scores compared
to other ensemble approaches such as CNN+LSTM, standalone XGBoost, and LightGBM.
Idhom et at. (2025) compared other hybrid models like LSTM, RNN, SVM, and traditional
Decision Tree and Random Forest, their CART with XGBoost model demonstrated superior
results across all performance metrics. Notably, recall improved from 88% to 90%, indicating
that the hybrid approach exhibited greater sensitivity in accurately identifying actual loan
defaults.
In comparative analyses of multiple hybrid structures, soft voting ensembles were found
to outperform hard voting combinations. A study that assessed SVM + LR, XGBoost + LR, and
23
RF + LR hybrid models demonstrated that soft voting consistently achieved better accuracy,
precision, recall, and F1-scores across all tests. Among the top-performing configurations,
combinations like XGB + RF, RF + LR, and RF + LGBM reached accuracies of up to 96.97%
and recall rates of 96%, underscoring the effectiveness of probabilistic ensemble integration for
balanced model output (Cao-Van et al., 2024).
Dey & Das (2022) reported that the hybrid LR-XGBoost model achieved superior
performance in slope instability assessment, obtaining an AUC of 80.5% on the training dataset,
surpassing the standalone LR (77.9%) and XGBoost (77.5%) models. On the validation dataset,
the hybrid also performed best with an AUC of 78.3%, compared to 76.8% for both LR and
XGBoost.
Overall, these studies highlight that hybrid ensemble models, particularly those utilizing
soft voting mechanisms and combinations like XGBoost, Decision Trees, and Logistic
Regression provide improved generalization, better handling of imbalanced data, and enhanced
sensitivity across various predictive tasks. This makes them highly suitable for domains
requiring both accuracy and reliability, such as healthcare, education, and agriculture.
Existing Contraceptive Discontinuation Prediction Systems
In the study conducted by Kebede et al. (2023), the researchers tested eight different
machine learning algorithms: logistic regression (LR), random forest (RF), k-nearest neighbors
(KNN), artificial neural network (ANN), support vector machine (SVM), naïve Bayes (NB),
XGBoost, and AdaBoost. The researchers used a sample of 6,737 women to predict
contraceptive discontinuation. Among these, the Random Forest model achieved the highest
performance, with an accuracy of 68% and Area Under the ROC Curve of 74%. The study also
identified key predictors of discontinuation, such as age, primary education, and the husband’s
desire for children, through feature importance analysis.
However, while the study by Kebede et al. (2023) successfully implemented multiple
predictive algorithms, it did not integrate these models into clinical decision-support or
24
mobile-based systems for contraceptive counseling. Furthermore, the authors noted several
limitations, including the difficulty of interpreting the strength of associations between predictors
and outcomes. Moreover, this study relies on a single algorithm rather than employing a hybrid
framework that balances interpretability and predictive performance. Hence, the present study
addresses these gaps by proposing a hybrid Decision Tree and XGBoost framework embedded
within a mobile application for discontinuation risk prediction, designed to function as a decision
support tool alongside WHO MEC recommendations.
Design Concept
The proposed system is an Android-based application designed to predict the likelihood
of contraceptive method discontinuation and assist healthcare providers and patients in making
informed and sustainable contraceptive choices. It utilizes a hybrid prediction model that
integrates CART Decision Tree and eXtreme Gradient Boosting models through a Soft Voting
Ensemble, balancing interpretability and predictive performance. The system also incorporates
the World Health Organization (WHO) Medical Eligibility Criteria (MEC) to ensure that all
recommendations are medically appropriate and safe. The architecture is composed of
interconnected modules that follow a cyclical process of prediction, recommendation, and
feedback.
Through the interface, healthcare providers input patient demographic, medical, and
reproductive data including age, parity, health conditions, contraceptive preferences. Data
preprocessing and feature engineering operations, such as cleaning, encoding, scaling, and
ensure consistent data suitable for machine learning. Selected features are stored and used to
train the DT and XGBoost models independently, with cross-validation and hyperparameter
tuning optimizing accuracy and generalization. The predictions from both models are then
combined through a Soft Voting Ensemble, producing a final risk score. The system presents
predicted discontinuation risk with an explainability layer using SHAP values to highlight key
contributing factors. Simultaneously, the WHO MEC assessment evaluates patient eligibility for
25
contraceptive methods, and the system integrates these assessments with model-based
predictions to generate recommendations that prioritize both medical safety and behavioral
sustainability. The decision support interface displays suitable contraceptive options, eligibility
categories, and discontinuation risks, enabling providers to counsel patients effectively with
evidence-based, personalized guidance.
A feedback mechanism allows providers to update patient outcomes, supporting model
retraining and continuous learning to improve predictive accuracy and system utility over time.
Overall, this design concept establishes an interpretable clinical decision-support framework
that integrates machine learning prediction with clinical eligibility assessment, identifies patients
at risk of contraceptive discontinuation, and continuously evolves to enhance both predictive
accuracy and clinical utility.

Multiple Constraints and Tradeoff Analysis
This section of the study aims at assessing the different algorithms appropriate for the
objective. The Accuracy, Recall, and F1-scores and AUC-ROC of the models will be evaluated
and documented in order to determine which model will be best chosen for the study. Accuracy
defines the overall percentage of correct predictions out of all samples advantageous when
classes are balanced along with its ease of interpretability. Recall is how well the model can
predict the positive cases correctly, a prioritized metric in situations where false positives can
result in impactful consequences. F1-score is the middle ground between recall and precision. A
higher recall means more false positives while a higher precision results more in false
negatives. The F1-score balances these two metrics. Lastly, AUC-ROC or Area Under the
Receiver Operating Characteristic curve evaluates the performance of the model in
distinguishing between the classes. A good model will result in an AUC close to 1.0 while a
model that is simply guessing will result in low AUC such as 0.2.

Accuracy refers to the proportion of correctly classified predictions out of the total

divided by the total number of classifications performed. A perfect model would achieve
an accuracy of 1.00 or 100%, indicating zero misclassifications.

the true positive instances, minimizing cases that are positive but classified as negative.

discontinuation prediction, the F1-Score is particularly useful because it evaluates how
well the model identifies users at risk while maintaining accuracy in its positive
predictions. A higher F1-Score indicates a better trade-off between recall and precision,

Design Trade-offs
The Decision Tree algorithm was selected for its simplicity, interpretability, and ease of
deployment in healthcare-related decision systems. A Decision Tree models decisions in a
hierarchical structure where internal nodes represent features, branches represent conditions,
and leaf nodes represent predicted outcomes. This transparency makes it highly suitable for
52
medical contexts where explainability is critical, allowing healthcare providers to understand why
the system assigns a particular risk classification. However, Decision Trees are prone to
overfitting, especially when dealing with complex, nonlinear relationships or noisy data.
Consequently, while a single Decision Tree offers interpretability, it often sacrifices predictive
performance and generalizability when used on heterogeneous datasets such as those
involving health behaviors and contraceptive use.
In contrast, the eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) algorithm was used for its
predictive power and robustness to data irregularities. XGBoost is an ensemble method based
on boosting, where multiple weak learners are combined sequentially to minimize prediction
errors. Each iteration focuses on correcting the mistakes of the previous one, resulting in a
model that can effectively capture complex patterns in data. This makes XGBoost particularly
advantageous for modeling multifactorial behaviors such as contraceptive discontinuation,
which may depend on subtle interactions among medical, demographic, and behavioral factors.
However, eXGBoost is computationally intensive, less interpretable, and requires extensive
hyperparameter tuning to prevent overfitting or bias.
To balance these trade-offs, the study implements a hybrid modeling approach that
combines the interpretability of Decision Tree and the predictive strength of XGBoost. In this
configuration, both models are trained using the same dataset, and their predictions are
aggregated through a voting mechanism to form a more stable and reliable classification of
contraceptive discontinuation risk. The Decision Tree contributes clear and interpretable
decision boundaries, while XGBoost enhances generalization and accuracy by capturing
nonlinear feature interactions. This hybrid strategy leverages the strengths of both algorithms
achieving a balance between explainability and performance which makes it an optimal design
for a clinical decision support system, where both model interpretability and predictive reliability
are essential for informed medical decision-making.

Trade-Off Analysis
Logistic Regression is a simple and interpretable algorithm that models the probability
of a binary outcome using a linear combination of input features. Its main advantage is ease of
implementation and transparency, making it suitable for healthcare applications where
explainability is important. However, LR assumes linear relationships between features and the
log-odds of the outcome, which can limit its predictive power for complex, non-linear patterns. It
is computationally efficient and robust for small datasets.
SVM (Support Vector Machine) is effective for classification tasks, particularly when
classes are separable in high-dimensional space. It can handle non-linear relationships, offering
flexibility beyond linear models. Its main strength is maximizing the margin between classes,
improving generalization. However, SVMs can be computationally intensive for large datasets,
sensitive to the choice of kernel and hyperparameters, and less interpretable than logistic
regression, which can limit their practical use in clinical decision-making.
Decision Trees offer clear and interpretable decision rules, making them particularly
valuable in healthcare settings where transparency and explainability are essential. They can
effectively handle both numerical and categorical data while capturing non-linear relationships
between variables. However, their main limitation lies in their tendency to overfit, especially
when the tree becomes too deep or complex, which can reduce their performance on new data.
.Random Forest is an ensemble method that builds multiple decision trees and
combines their predictions, improving accuracy and robustness. It handles non-linearities and
feature interactions well while reducing overfitting compared to single decision trees. The
trade-offs include higher computational cost and reduced interpretability compared to individual
trees. This algorithm is particularly suitable when high predictive performance is prioritized over
model transparency.
XGBoost (eXtreme Gradient Boosting) is a high-performance gradient boosting
algorithm that sequentially builds decision trees to minimize prediction errors. It is highly

number of hyperparameters requiring tuning, limited interpretability, and longer training time
compared to simpler algorithms. XGBoost is ideal when maximizing predictive performance is

Algorithm Implementation
The hybrid model for this study will use Decision Tree along with eXtreme Gradient
Boosting to perform a binary classification between discontinued (1) and continued (0). The
models are scored based on the recall metric as the system will prioritize avoiding false
62
negatives than false positives as the former can result in consequential situations. Combining
the two algorithms will require the use of ensemble techniques, specifically VotingClassifier
sklearn.ensemble. The study used soft voting that averages the probabilities of each classifier
and selects the output with the highest probability. This improves accuracy, robustness, and
reduces overfitting. Hypertuning additionally includes 10-fold cross validation which as the name
suggests divides the input data into 10 different groups. This method puts the majority of the
data for training which mitigate biases.
Decision Tree
This algorithm is a tree-like structure that is composed of the root node where the
dataset is whole, branches that connect all the nodes, inner/decision nodes that contain the
rules which splits the data, and leaf nodes where the predictions are located. The advantage of
this algorithm lies in its simplicity which improves the interpretability of the model.
Best splits are measured using the Gini impurity and Entropy. The former is a measure
of how well the data is split into two distinct categories, the lower the Gini the better the split.
The latter is also known as the disorder in the tree. It is a criteria that the decision tree tries to
minimize by splitting the data based on the most important features.
Pruning limits the decision tree to a specific depth. The deeper the tree the more
chance for overfitting from noise in data. A rule of thumb in determining if a tree needs pruning
is when the training accuracy greatly surpasses that of testing accuracy.

eXtreme Gradient Boosting
XGBoost got its name from its chosen ensemble technique. Boosting is an ensemble
method that sequentially trains the model. Each model boosts the weakness of its predecessor.
XGBoost is an optimized implementation of gradient boosting that uses decision trees as its
base learners. The cycle of this algorithm is simple, each tree will be trained, error is calculated,
and its weaknesses are passed onto the next decision tree model. At the end, the predictions
are summed up and given as the answer.

Training data includes randomly sampling the training data. The yellow circles shown in

base learning model. Regularization happens during this part of the training making sure it
doesn’t overfit.
CART (classification and regression trees) represented in blue colored borders, tries to
predict the outcome based on the input. Since this is a sequential training only the first decision

tree model will be trained normally. The rest are trained using the errors of the previous training
model.
Weighting increase is done by giving importance to the classes that are misclassified.
Note that this is firstly done for the second decision tree model as the first model does not have
any predecessor.
Prediction is done every time CART. At this part, loss function is performed to determine
how well the model performed or more specifically where the model performed worst. This is
done iteratively until performance stops on improving after a number of estimators. Lastly, the
predictions of all decision trees are summed up for the actual output.
Data Preparation
The raw dataset was collected from NDHS, it contains about 5493 rows and 31 columns
including the dependent variable. The first column being “CASEID” was dropped due to its
irrelevancy in giving information for the algorithms. The “HUSBAND_AGE” column was
discovered to be an object data type which was made into a float, same with the other features
that are in object data type. After conversion, the categorical columns are processed through
one-hot encoding. The columns “KIDS” and “RESPONDENT_AGE”, didn’t reflect categorical
values, hence the column was left as is. The dependent variable was created based on the
“REASON_DISCONTINUED” column. From this, a new column named “DISCONTINUED”
showing 1s and 0s was formed indicating that if there is a reason then the client discontinued.
Lastly, the dataset was split into training and testing divisions where 80% was for training and
the rest for testing.
Model Training
Four algorithms were selected for this study: Logistic Regression, Random Forest
Classification, Decision Tree, and XGBoost. These models were chosen as they are the
common algorithms used for classification training. Logistic Regression was included because
66
of its simplicity and interpretability while the Random Forest Classifier works as an ensemble
method, combining multiple decision trees to enhance the predictive capability. Decision Tree is
a tree-like structure algorithm commonly tasked to do both classification and regression.
XGBoost on the other hand is a gradient boosting framework that is chosen due to its
recognizable performance in classification problems. Hyperparameters were implemented later
to fine-tune through different trial runs for optimizing the algorithms ensuring that the study
received the best model at its best. The models were trained using the data and hypertuned
using grid search cross-validation with 10 folds for optimization, a vital step in deciding which of
these can perform well in a hybrid model. From the top performing algorithms based on recall,
different combinations of 2 were tested using stacked and voting classifiers with each being fully
optimized.
Model Evaluation
After training the models are evaluated using accuracy, AUC-ROC, recall, and
f1-scores. This is done by comparing the outcomes of the predictions with the actual results in
the testing data. Accuracy shows the measure of overall correctness of the models, AUC-ROC
assessed the reliability of the model in distinguishing the difference in outcomes, recall helped in
determining the capability of the models to capture actual discontinuation outcomes, and
f1-score acts as a balance for both precision and recall. Preprocessing, training, and evaluation
are fairly and equally performed for all the models ensuring that there is no bias for any
algorithms. The evaluation part of the study is vital in ensuring that the hybrid model picked for
the study was appropriate for this case.
Testing and Operating Procedure
The system will be tested using Python to evaluate model performance and ensure
accurate prediction of contraceptive discontinuation risk. Tools such as scikit-learn will be

applied for model training, validation, and testing, while Pandas and NumPy will be used for

Model Validation Strategy
To ensure the reliability and generalizability of the predictive model for contraceptive
discontinuation risk, appropriate validation strategies are employed. These strategies are
selected to address the classification nature of the problem while maintaining fairness across
different user groups and ensuring the accuracy of predicted probabilities. The following
approaches are applied:
Stratified k-Fold Cross-Validation (10-fold). This technique divides the dataset into ten
folds while preserving the distribution of the target classes in each fold. It allows every data
point to be used for both training and validation, providing a more robust and unbiased estimate
of model performance.
Calibration Analysis. Since the model outputs discontinuation risk as probabilities,
calibration is assessed to evaluate how well these predicted probabilities reflect actual
outcomes. Methods such as calibration plots and Brier scores are used to verify the reliability of
risk predictions.
Hold-out Test Set Evaluation. A separate portion of the dataset is set aside as a final
test set, unseen during training and cross-validation. This ensures that the reported
performance metrics reflect the model’s true predictive ability on new, unseen data.

Comprehensive Testing Methodology
The proposed system will undergo a testing methodology to ensure its functionality,
reliability, and alignment with the needs of both patients and healthcare providers. The testing
approach will follow four levels: unit testing, integration testing, system testing, and acceptance
testing, each designed to validate the system at increasing levels of complexity.
Unit Testing. At this level, individual modules of the system will be tested in isolation to
confirm that they perform as expected. This includes testing the encoding of WHO MEC rules,
the module for discontinuation risk prediction, and the display of contraceptive information such
as price, usage guidelines, and expected outcomes. Each function will be validated against its
design specifications to ensure correctness.
Integration Testing. Once individual modules are verified, integration testing will be
conducted to examine how components work together. For instance, the linkage between the
eligibility module (MEC-based filtering) and the discontinuation risk prediction model will be
tested to ensure accurate and seamless data transfer. Similarly, the integration of backend
computations with the user interface will be assessed for consistency and proper information
flow.
System Testing. At this stage, the complete system will be tested as a whole to validate
compliance with functional and non-functional requirements. This includes confirming the
accuracy of contraceptive recommendations, the responsiveness of the mobile application, and
the consistency of predictive outputs across different user scenarios. Performance metrics such
as processing time, reliability under concurrent users, and stability across devices will also be
evaluated.
Acceptance Testing. The final stage will involve end-user validation to ensure the
system meets real-world expectations. Both healthcare providers and potential patients will
participate in testing sessions, where they will interact with the tool in simulated healthcare
69
settings. Their feedback will be gathered through surveys and direct observation, focusing on
usability, clarity of recommendations, and practical value in clinical decision-making. Acceptance
testing ensures that the tool is both technically sound and practically useful before deployment.
Project Evaluation
The evaluation of the system will be conducted using the ISO/IEC 25010 model, which
outlines the key quality characteristics for assessing software systems. This internationally
recognized standard evaluates the functionality, reliability, usability, and overall quality of the
system, making it ideal for a healthcare-focused mobile application that deals with sensitive
reproductive health data. End-users will be engaged in the evaluation process to assess
usability, accessibility, and overall experience within the application. Meanwhile, technical
experts will review performance efficiency, maintainability, and system security. To ensure the
medical accuracy and clinical reliability of the system’s contraceptive recommendations and
predictions of discontinuation risk, these elements will be validated by qualified medical
professionals. Through this multi-level evaluation approach, ContraceptIQ will be user-centered,
technically robust, and clinically trustworthy.
The following criteria will be used for the evaluation:
Functional Suitability. The system is designed to address the specific needs of both
patients and healthcare providers by delivering accurate, relevant, and reliable information on
contraceptive methods. It integrates the WHO MEC eligibility guidelines with discontinuation risk
predictions to ensure that recommendations are both medically appropriate. The system also
provides essential details such as the cost, usage instructions, and possible side effects of each
method. For patients, it serves as an accessible source of trustworthy information to support
informed decision-making, while for healthcare providers, it functions as a clinical
decision-support tool that enhances the effectiveness of contraceptive counseling.
Performance Efficiency. The application will be optimized to handle data processing
quickly and efficiently, ensuring that the discontinuation risk levels of each contraceptive method
70
are provided without delays. Since the system will be used in healthcare settings where time is
critical, performance efficiency will support smooth consultations for providers and a responsive
experience for patients using the mobile platform.
Interaction Capability. The system is designed with usability in mind, offering an
intuitive interface for both patients and doctors. Patients will be able to navigate the tool easily to
review contraceptive options, while providers will have access to more detailed inputs and
results for clinical use. Features such as clear instructions, accessible language, and structured
data presentation will ensure that users can interact effectively with the tool regardless of their
level of technical expertise.
Maintainability. To ensure long-term usability, the system will be developed with
modular and adaptable components, allowing updates to be made when new contraceptive
guidelines, pricing data, or risk models become available. This is particularly important as
medical recommendations evolve, and the system must remain accurate and relevant. Regular
updates will also allow continuous improvement and incorporation of feedback from patients and
providers.
Compatibility. The system will be developed to operate smoothly on mobile devices,
ensuring accessibility for its intended users. By being mobile-supported, the tool can be
conveniently integrated into healthcare workflows and remain functional in both public and
private health facilities. This mobile compatibility is particularly important in reaching diverse
users, from patients in rural areas to doctors in urban hospitals, where mobile access is often
the most practical option.
